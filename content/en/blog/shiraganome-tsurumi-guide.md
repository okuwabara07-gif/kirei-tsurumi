## Understanding the Cause of Gray Hair Leads to Optimal Solutions

There's much about gray hair that isn't widely understood. Many people assume "if I have gray hair, it's just because I'm getting older, so there's nothing I can do about it." But that's not entirely accurate. The causes of gray hair are complex, involving not just aging, but also stress, nutritional deficiencies, and lifestyle habits all working together.

Yamamoto explains: "Among our clients in their 40s who experience increased gray hair, we often notice a common pattern—it coincides with periods of high work-related stress. Additionally, this is also the time approaching menopause, so hormonal changes also play a significant role."

So what's the most effective approach once gray hair begins to increase? The key is thinking about "how to maintain your hair's texture going forward" at the same time you color it. In the short term, you want to even out your color, but in the long term, you don't want to damage your hair. Balancing these two goals is the central theme of choosing the right gray hair coloring approach.

Interestingly, many clients who've tried at-home self-coloring report: "I thought it would be fine at first, but three months later, my hair was clearly damaged." This happens because, unlike professional application, the processing time and application method at home often aren't optimal. That's why, once gray hair starts becoming noticeable, it's crucial to understand "what kind of professional treatment should I actually be receiving?"

## Understanding the Difference Between Organic and Traditional Color

When choosing a gray hair coloring service, have you come across the term "organic"? More Yokohama salons are offering this option, but few people truly understand what makes it different.

With 15 years of experience as a colorist, Yamamoto specializes in organic coloring. "The biggest difference between traditional chemical color and organic color is the degree of damage to the scalp. Chemical color provides quick results with vibrant color, but in the process, it can cause strong irritation to the scalp. Organic color, on the other hand, uses plant-derived ingredients, so it can significantly reduce the burden on the scalp."

Clients at Tsurumi who've chosen organic coloring often report: "There was almost no irritation during the treatment" and "The color is subtle and natural-looking—people tell me it looks very natural." To be frank, organic coloring typically costs more than chemical coloring. However, when you consider it a long-term investment in "buying the health of your hair and scalp," it's not expensive at all.

## Adjusting Your Treatment Method Based on the Amount and Pattern of Your Gray Hair

"Gray hair coloring is the same everywhere, right?" Many people might think so, but that's actually not the case. The optimal treatment method varies significantly depending on how much gray hair you have and how it's distributed.

Among our clients, some have "very little gray hair on the ends, but it's concentrated at the roots," while others have "gray hair scattered throughout." Yamamoto explains: "When gray hair is concentrated only at the roots, we can handle it with a 'roots touch-up,' coloring only that area. However, when there's gray hair throughout, we need to color the entire head. But since the ends were colored previously, applying new color to the ends can make the color too dark. That's why we need to use different products for the roots and the ends."

Many people who've done self-coloring at home have probably "colored their entire head with the same product every time." As a result, the ends become too dark or overly damaged. Professional salons make these fine adjustments at each appointment, which is why the condition of the hair is dramatically different.

As a concrete example, one of our clients at Tsurumi who receives monthly root touch-ups reports: "The condition of my ends is surprisingly healthy." After switching from self-coloring to professional care just two months in, the texture of her hair changed completely. When you entrust your care to a professional who can assess the amount and distribution of your gray hair, you can maintain the ideal state: "gray hair is unnoticeable, and hair remains healthy."

## Coloring Intervals and Color Selection Determine Your Youthful Appearance

Once you begin gray hair coloring, the next things to consider are "how often should I touch up?" and "what color should I choose?" These two factors significantly influence how youthful and vibrant you look.

Generally speaking, gray hair grows about 0.5 to 1 cm per month. That means after four weeks, about 1 to 1.3 cm of new gray hair appears at the roots. When people start to notice regrowth varies individually, but Yamamoto recommends "root touch-ups every 4 to 6 weeks." She explains: "Considering the hair growth cycle, this interval provides the best balance between scalp and hair health. If you do it too frequently, you increase the burden on the scalp; if you wait too long, clients start to feel self-conscious."

Color selection is equally important. To be frank, many people choose a color close to black simply because they want to hide gray hair, but this can backfire. Pure black can make your face appear darker depending on your skin tone, and the strong contrast with gray hair can actually make the regrowth more noticeable.

In actual consultations, it's important to diagnose "what color suits you best" together. For many clients in their 40s living in Tsurumi Ward, Yokohama, lighter browns or dark browns often suit their skin tone and hair type well. A professional colorist will look at your entire face and suggest: "This color will brighten your complexion."

## Post-Treatment Home Care Determines Color Longevity and Hair Health

While professional treatment is essential, what you do afterward significantly affects how long your color lasts and the overall health of your hair. Many clients don't realize this, so they think their color is fading quickly—but it's actually "the difference in aftercare."

Immediately after gray hair coloring, your hair's cuticles are in an open state. That's why it's recommended to avoid shampooing for three days if possible. If it's absolutely necessary, washing with lukewarm water gently and rinsing with cool water can help close the cuticles.

Choosing the right shampoo and treatment products is also crucial. Yamamoto strongly recommends: "If you want to prevent color fading, use a color-safe shampoo. Regular shampoos are too harsh and wash away color pigment over time."

Additionally, a weekly hair mask is also effective. When you receive organic coloring treatment, using this care regimen in particular provides benefits like "your hair maintains its shine" and "color lasts longer." As an example, some of our clients at Tsurumi have found that "once I started being mindful about home care, I could extend the time between treatments to six weeks without worrying about color fading."

To be frank, developing the habit of putting effort into post-treatment care leads in the long run to two key benefits: "you can reduce how often you visit the salon" and "your hair looks more beautiful."
